▸ Case Study — ReadZam

Teaching a child to read
by teaching them to hear.

How we positioned a free Zambian literacy app around the one thing parents fear most — and the one thing competitors miss.

01 — Diagnosis

The brief

ReadZam is a free literacy app built for Zambian children. It teaches reading through phonics — sounds first, then words, then sentences. The audience: parents who are worried, and teachers who are stretched thin.

Primary Audience

Zambian parents of early readers (ages 4–9) and primary school teachers in both urban and rural areas.

Core Differentiator

ReadZam teaches reading through sounds (phonics) — not rote memorisation. Most Zambian children are taught to memorise words whole, not decode them.

Emotional Trigger

Fear. Not frustration. Parents aren't annoyed their child can't read — they are afraid. Afraid of dropout. Afraid of a future closed off before it starts.

Barrier to Action

Trust. Parents have seen apps before. Free promises that go nowhere. The copy must make them believe this one is different — and that they can use it today.

Analyst's Note The instinct is to lead with "free" and "fun." That's wrong for this audience. A parent who fears their child will drop out of school doesn't need fun. They need to believe this works. The copy must earn trust before it earns a download.

Section — How It Works
Reading is a science.
We teach it like one.

Most children who struggle to read aren't slow. They were never taught the sounds that letters make. ReadZam fixes that — one sound at a time.

Step 1 — Sounds. Every letter has a sound. We teach them all, with audio, clearly.

Step 2 — Words. Sounds become words. Children blend them together until it clicks.

Step 3 — Reading. Words become sentences. Sentences become stories. Stories become confidence.

No guessing. No memorisation. Just the proven method — made free and accessible for every Zambian child.

Strategic Note — Why This Headline Works "Your child isn't behind" immediately disarms the guilt and shame a parent carries. It reframes the problem (no sounds taught) without blaming the child or the parent. "They just haven't heard the sounds yet" introduces the phonics mechanism without using the word "phonics" — which means nothing to most parents. The subheadline then delivers the promise, the method, and the price point in one sentence.

Welcome email — Parent sequence

Email 1 of 3. Sent immediately after a parent signs up or shares their contact. Goal: deepen trust and get the child's first session started.

Strategic Note — Why "You're not hoping anymore. You're starting." Parents of struggling readers live in a state of suspended hope — they try things and wait to see. This line does two things: it validates that emotional state (hoping) without dwelling on it, then immediately moves them into agency (starting). It closes the email on momentum, not sentiment.
04 — Lab Notes

What this copy is designed to do

⟳
Conversion goal: fear → belief → first session Every line of this copy moves a worried parent one step closer to opening the app with their child. The headline removes shame. The body copy explains the mechanism simply. The CTA removes all friction ("no download, no sign-up"). The email deepens the relationship and drives the first real action.
◎
Why phonics is never mentioned by name "Phonics" is a technical term. It means something to educators and nothing to most parents. This copy explains the mechanism (sounds → words → reading) without requiring the reader to know the vocabulary. This is the difference between copy written by a subject expert and copy written for a buyer.
